I spent the last days of 2020 in reflection and quiet work. The days were aimless and rainy, with dark clouds brooding over a titanium gray sky.

I did a couple of things in those silent, languid days. I made a slide deck for an upcoming talk. I had been asked to give a talk on building a fulfilling life while having a career you can enjoy. What better way to write this talk - I thought - than to reflect on my own life and lessons. I opened my journal and started writing longform. I scribbled a list of precepts - reminders to myself, really - that I can share with my audience for the talk. Some of them came from lessons in my own life, some from books I have read or talks I have watched. I hope you find them useful too. Here are they are...




Choose what you care about.

Have you read this wonderful book "The Subtle Art of Not Giving a F*ck?" Don't be fooled by it's title. The book is not an argument for utter thoughtlessness about *everything* in your life. There are clearly things that you should care about. For me - as 2020 made abundantly clear - these are my health, my significant relationships, and doing good work that matters. But by nature, I care about too many things around me, and that's what the book preaches against. Over the course of my life, though, I have learned to be choosier. I have learned to ask questions like: Does it really matter what my random co-worker thinks about me? Why should I care what my aunt thinks about my life choices?

Choose what you spend your mental calories on - it can free your mind.


Mastery is a process, not a state.

Many of us are in high-octane professions or have bold goals. Our work typically calls for a level of excellence that we are not born with. Becoming great at work will take time. I have told the story of my early days at Microsoft before. This process of "noticing my gaps -> resolving to learn -> learning -> getting feedback" is cyclical and has been ever-present in my career. Whether it is coding or leadership or speaking or writing, being at peace with not being an instant virtuoso - and reminding myself that "this is good enough, for now" - has helped ease my mind.


Trust that you will figure it out.

In the summer of 2016, I decided to walk the Swiss Alps, from Chamonix to Zermatt, along a well-known path called the Haute Route. It took me about two weeks to walk the entire 100+ miles, scaling multiple passes. It was one of most wondrous things I have ever done. Night skies strewn with a million stars, massive blue glaciers tumbled over mountainsides, rumbling waterfalls, forests thick with trees, and winds so strong they could push you down if you don't take care. The walk was also one of the most challenging things I have done, completely beyond my comfort zone.



When I decided to do it, I was beset by doubts. Will I have the strength to climb such high mountains? Will the scrambles be too much for my wobbly footing and frail ankles? I write about one particularly dreadful section here. It was a goat path winding up a mountain that felt like a heap of rocks. With every step you took, your feet sank and slithered, and you prayed to all the gods you believed in and the ones you didn't believe in too. But I summited it, despite my self-doubts and fears. I asked for help from my husband. I watched where I planted my feet. I used rocks as leverage to pull myself up. It was slow going and nerve-wracking, but I figured it out.

It is the same deal with work. To do big, scary things - in life or work - you need to know deep down that you will figure it out. Because you will.


Every endeavor can teach you something. Be curious.

It takes little experiments and baby steps to build that trust in yourself. When we build software products, in my team, we experiment a lot. We make little hypotheses and test them out. We learn. That is how we know when to build on and what to alter. Likewise, think of every activity you pursue - every interaction you have, every meeting you attend - as a little experiment that can help you learn something. Not with a goal to impress or get everything down pat.

One of my mentors told me once to "collect data with every interaction". Approach your work with that scientist-like mindset and you will find that you look forward to many previously frightful activities.


Do not dwell.



In 2018, I was asked to demo a few Microsoft technologies to a packed arena of 20000+ IT Professionals. I had never done anything like that before, and I was nervous. Understandably, I wanted to nail it. My demo was 30 minutes long, split into two segments. I was in the middle of my second segment when I realized something was wrong with my demo. My prepared words were spilling out of my mouth, but what I expected on the screen didn't materialize. The product team had just deployed something that caused my script to become irrelevant. I stumbled.

Even today when I watch the video clip of it, I zoom in, within a microsecond, to where I failed. I cringe. That's how much I can dwell on my failures. But when I got off the stage that day, feeling like a child who had just dropped her ice cream cone on dirt, I went straight to my team to say I was sorry. I had failed them. I had failed the demo. But my team didn't even notice my fumble. They were puzzled what I was talking about. They thought everything had gone as planned. Not just them, but no one that watched me present that day pointed to my gaffe. The moral: do not dwell on your screw-ups. Sure, failures are important learning opportunities, but there is no use in dwelling on what's happened.


You have a right to a vision and voice of your own.

How many times have you wanted to say something in a meeting, and you didn't? You either felt nervous about opening your mouth or that your perspective was not that important. Know that your voice is important in your workplace. What's more, you have a right to be there, a right to voice your perspective, a vision that is all your own even if it is not the most popular one. Here are some ways in which you will help calm those meeting nerves.


Do your work. The outcome and people's reactions are not your concern.

When I was growing up, my father often told me to do my best and leave the rest to God. I like a variation of that aphorism now: Do your work, do not concern yourself with the outcome. Focusing on my work - not on how it is received or how it is going to succeed or fail - has helped me not just be happier but produce higher quality work. In fact, I do this a lot with my writing. I write because I enjoy writing, I have something to say, I have a right to say it, and I believe it will help others. What others think of what I say, whether they agree or not, whether it is well-received or not - these are not my concern.


Make time for important things.



First, make a list of things that are important to you - work, health, relationships, fun. Anything else you're doing that doesn't contribute to what is important to you - like binge watching that show on Netflix, doomscrolling through Twitter, gossiping with a relative - cut it from your life. We often feel like we do not have time to do everything. Here is the thing: you don't need to do everything you're doing today. If you are intentional about where you're spending your time, you will magically mine minutes throughout the day that you can use for important things in your life. Mining minutes has been an important insight for me. These days, I don't go anywhere without my book, for example. Reading is important to me, and I know that I can mine minutes while I waiting on the parking lot or the lobby of dentist to read. Find five minutes here, ten minutes there, to do the things you want to do.


Do not take yourself TOO seriously.

I am a serious person by nature. My life has shaped me to be to be one. Of course, I joke and I laugh, but only occasionally am I truly goofing around. But still, I remind myself that most of what I do and say and write and make will disappear in less than fifty years. Not to be too dark, but given enough time, oblivion is inevitable for most of us. So why take yourself too seriously if you are here for just for a blip of time? If everything is temporary, you can afford to play and even mess your hair a little. Give yourself that permission.


It's ok to be imperfect. It makes you human.



A very good friend of mine, who is a master storyteller, finishes her slides the morning of her talk. She hates rehearsing. I, on the other hand, obsess over what I have to say and how I say it. I come from a long line of people who didn't like to "get laughed at" by their neighbors and family, so aiming for perfection is as easy as mixing salt and water for me. It is not just me, though. Many luminaries that I admire - from Melinda Gates to Angela Merkel to Michelle Obama - are famous over-preparers. Christine LaGarde, the president of European Central Bank once said this about her preparations.


When we work on a particular matter, we will work the file inside, outside, sideways, backwards, historically, genetically, and geographically. We want to be completely on top of everything, and we want to understand it all, and we don't want to be fooled by somebody else.

Financial Times, July 2019


I have nothing against preparation. I continue to be someone who likes to prepare, especially for important meetings and events. But I also know that overpreparing can kill your ability to think on your feet. More importantly, it can make you anxious and lose valuable time. In the world of software development, we say that Done is better than perfect. It is true for life too. Remember that you are only human, and people know that. Most people are not expecting perfection from you. And no one is intently watching what you say and do. They are just caught up in their own lives.



As we leave 2020 behind, and start a fresh new year, it is a great moment for us to write reminders for ourselves that will help us move forward. These reminders act as guiding principles as well as help us deal with disempowering ideas in our head. What does your list look like?
